## 6. WHAT THE PRINT OBJECT CARRIES THAT THE WEB SECTIONS DON'T (for the DCD build list)

1. **The Reference Compendium is print-only furniture** — 32 pages of desk-reference lore (lunation table, ice thickness, name origins) that exists as scattered articles on the web but as a *bound reference block* only in print. The "back-of-book compendium" is a distinct product grammar: DCD's equivalent would be a static, dense, always-there reference surface, not more feed.
2. **The regional forecast section is the print purchase-driver** (~30 pages, pp.~202–232) — the web only teases seasonal summaries and sells the detail ("full forecast in the print edition"). The print object monetizes *withheld resolution*. DCD inverts this honestly: full resolution always, receipts always.
3. **Page-anchored ritual**: reviewers cite the book BY PAGE (p.97, p.202, p.230) — the fixed pagination across years (weather always ~97, regions always ~202) is itself an interface: readers navigate by muscle memory. Web nav can't replicate this; a stable, numbered layout can.
4. **The claim-verdict pages sit adjacent in print** (How We Predict → How Accurate Was Last Winter → the new forecast, one section) — the modern form still fuses claim and self-grade at the section level, even if generously scored. The web scatters these. DCD's court-on-the-claim-card is the faithful upgrade.
5. **The ad well is still the book's economics** and its most dated feature — reader complaints about ED/cannabis/septic ads are the 2026 version of the 1950 PEN PALS conscience box, minus the conscience. The form tolerates commerce woven into reference; it no longer fences it.
6. **The hole** — the physical mount-point ritual survives at $40K/yr against all economics. The lesson isn't the hole; it's that the publisher pays real money annually to preserve a zero-feature *ritual affordance*.
7. **Hunting/fishing law is gone from the modern book** — the 1950 almanac's `hunt_seasons` table has no modern print heir. Nobody occupies that ground with the almanac's grammar (dated, fitted, caveated). DCD already holds the data.

## 7. FARMERS' ALMANAC (Lewiston) 2026 — the lighter pass

- **The 2026 edition (published Aug 2025) was announced in November 2025 as the FINAL print edition** after 208 years (est. 1818). Editor Sandi Duncan: decision made "with a heavy heart." Website was to stay up only through Dec 31, 2025. [M] (PopSci, AP-syndicated coverage)
- **Final-edition contents:** winter 2025–26 forecast — "a wild ride," dramatic temperature swings, "**chill, snow, repeat**," coldest in Northern Plains→New England; cover art showed **farmhouse AND cityscape** (urban-reader pivot). Traditional sections to the end: 7-zone long-range forecasts (pseudonymous "Caleb Weatherbee," formula locked in a black box), Best Days, fishing calendar, gardening-by-the-moon, trivia/jokes, natural remedies. [M]
- **The save:** January 2026 — acquired by newly formed **Farmers' Almanac LLC** (Brooklyn Heights, NY), owned by **Tim & John Konrad / Unofficial Networks** (ski/outdoor digital media). **Peter Geiger → Editor Emeritus**; Geiger family retains formal connection. farmersalmanac.com stays live with archive intact; relaunch announced for **March 2026, digital-first**; print "plans underway to revive and expand the cherished annual print edition in future volumes" — no date or format committed. [M] (WBUR, Maine Public, Sun Journal, farmersalmanac.com's own announcement)
- **Market note:** the November shutdown news caused a run on the *Old* Farmer's Almanac (consumers conflated the two; OFA had to publish "still going strong" statements and saw a sales surge). Two-century-old brand confusion is still a live market force. [M] (Valley News, NHBR)
- **Net for DCD:** as of today there is exactly **one almanac print object being produced** (OFA). The FA's print lane is a promise, not a product. The "almanac form" on racks in fall 2026 = OFA No. 235 alone.
