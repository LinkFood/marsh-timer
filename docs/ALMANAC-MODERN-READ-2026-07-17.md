## 1. State of the two publishers, July 2026

**Old Farmer's Almanac (OFA)** — alive and unchanged in kind: the 2026 edition (234th year, on sale Aug 26 2025) is a 288-page paperback, **$8.99 cover / $9.95 store classic**, "distinguished by the hole in the upper left corner," sold at mass retail (the Home Depot checkout copy is this book). The press release leads with the winter map ("**MOSTLY MILD, WITH POCKETS OF WILD**") and the 80%-accuracy claim; the bullet-list of contents is all feature articles (America's 250th, "galactic garbage," "Demystifying the Man of the Signs") — **weather + features are the marketed identity; the astronomical spine isn't marketed at all**, it's assumed. Website is fully free and ad-supported (Raptive/AdThrive programmatic, no login paywall anywhere); monetization = print book as the year-gate + $6.95 page-flip Online Edition + $4.95 à-la-carte 12-month forecast PDF + clubs ($27.92 Essentials / $54.97 Gardening / $109.97 "1792 Society") + $35/yr Garden Planner SaaS + relentless email capture.

**Farmers' Almanac (FA)** — died and came back as a website. Timeline (verified): **Nov 6 2025** the Geiger family announces the 208-year-old publication ends with the 2026 edition; **Jan 28 2026** bought by **Tim Konrad** (founder of Unofficial Networks, a family ski/outdoors digital-media company) via Farmers' Almanac LLC, Brooklyn Heights NY; **March 2026** site relaunches as a "digital reboot… a living, breathing publication with fresh, daily content." **No print edition currently exists** — print revival promised for "future volumes" only. The relaunch ported the old site nearly wholesale: same 7 US forecast zones, same lunar calendars, same $13.99/yr All-Access price, same Caleb Weatherbee mythology (now lore, not byline — new forecasts are "Farmers' Almanac Staff"), even the old "©2026 Almanac Publishing Co." in the footer. Peter Geiger persists as Editor Emeritus contributor (updated "20 Signs of a Hard Winter" June 2026).

**The market read:** in 2026 an outdoor-media digital publisher judged the almanac *form* — daily lunar/weather/lore ritual content — worth acquiring and running as a daily website with a $13.99 subscription. That is exactly DCD's lane, bet on with money, this year.

## 3. Post-1950 inventions — now CONFIRMED from both ends

The 1950 primary read hypothesized these were later inventions; the modern read confirms each and dates the pressure that created them:

1. **The regional long-range forecast system** — 1950: one sentence (Boston − 1 day/time zone). 2026: 18 US + 7 Canadian regions at OFA, 7 zones + AK + 5 CA at FA, with 60/30-day free windows and paid year views. The region map is the modern *product*, not the method.
2. **Full moon names as a feature** — absent 1950; now a 12-page monthly franchise with tribal attributions, bibliographies, and de-attributions. The single most press-reprinted almanac artifact is younger than 1950.
3. **The Best Days timetable** — 1950 footer line → 38-activity × 12-month database, per-activity SEO pages, the year view as paywall. The form's biggest mutation.
4. **The accuracy percentage** — 1950 graded itself with dated event-anecdotes and an admitted miss; the decimal self-score (86.1%) is modern theater layered on the same one-surface claim+verdict grammar.
5. **The astrology reversal** — 1950's glossary: zodiac = basis of "some astrology — and superstition." 2026's Man of Signs page: "**not mere superstition but a trusted guide**," staff astrologist on roster. The fence didn't erode; it was inverted.
6. **The graded fishing day** (Poor/Fair/Good/Best per day, solunar overlay) — 1950 footer line → rating table.
7. **The hole** — not on the 1950 copy; today it's a marketed product feature ("distinguished by the hole in the upper left corner").
8. **Per-ZIP localization** — replaced the key-letter correction system entirely on the web; the 1950 apparatus survives only in print [print, unverified].

**What survived 1950 → 2026 unchanged:** the claim+verdict-on-one-surface grammar; the day-range forecast idiom ("Sunny, then t-storms; hot"); the wink; the persona (Weatherwise/Weatherbee); the daily-panel marginalia (puzzles, oddities, feasts); ads as load-bearing structure.
